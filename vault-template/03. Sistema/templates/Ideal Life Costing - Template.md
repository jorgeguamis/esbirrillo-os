This framework is adapted from Tim Ferriss's "Dreamlining" exercise in *The 4-Hour Workweek*.

The premise: most people overestimate what their ideal life costs. Once you know the actual number, you can make clear decisions about how much you need to earn, save, or build.

This is not about minimalism. It's about precision.

---

## Instructions

1. Describe your ideal lifestyle in concrete terms (not "financial freedom" — but "what you'd actually do daily")
2. Cost out each component monthly and annually
3. Add a 20-30% buffer for unknowns
4. Compare to your current income or net worth
5. Make decisions based on the gap

## Part 5: Decision-Making

This exercise exists to help you make better decisions.

### Decision Framework

When evaluating opportunities, ask:

**Does this move me toward the required income/net worth?**
- If yes, by how much?
- If no, why am I considering it?

**Does this align with the lifestyle I actually want?**
- If yes, great.
- If no, am I chasing someone else's definition of success?

**What's the fastest path to closing the gap?**
- Build and scale the current business?
- Sell the business and invest proceeds?
- Transition to a different business model?
- Reduce the cost of your ideal life?

---

### Strategic Options

Based on your gap, you have a few strategic paths:

**Option 1: Reduce the cost of your ideal life**
- What can you remove without reducing quality of life?
- What's lifestyle inflation vs actual values?

**Option 2: Increase income/growth rate**
- What levers can you pull to grow faster?
- What would 2x revenue in 2 years require?

**Option 3: Change your business model**
- Is there a higher-leverage way to generate the income you need?
- Should you build for sale, or build for cash flow?

**Option 4: Redefine success**
- Is the ideal life you described actually what you want?
- Or is it what you think you should want?

## Notes on This Exercise

**This is not about deprivation.**
If your ideal life costs $2M/year, own that. Just know the number and what's required to get there.

**This is not static.**
Your ideal life will change. Redo this exercise every 1-2 years.

**This is not just about money.**
The point is clarity. Once you know the number, you can stop wondering "how much is enough?" and start making decisions.

**This reveals priorities.**
If you say you value freedom but your ideal life requires $1M/year in income, you're designing a cage. If you say you value luxury but your ideal life costs $80k/year, you're undershooting.

The goal is alignment: between what you say you want, what your life actually costs, and how you're building toward it.

Do this exercise honestly, and a lot of decisions become obvious.
